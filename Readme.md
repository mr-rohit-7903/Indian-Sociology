*⏱ ~1 min 45 sec*

Good morning/afternoon, everyone.

We are Group 12, and today we'll be presenting on the **Impact of Indian Science, Humanities, and Technology on Transformative Development and SDG-Based Goals.**

India stands at a unique crossroads — a civilisation thousands of years old, now racing toward becoming a developed nation by 2047. But what does "development" really mean? Is it just GDP? Just infrastructure? We argue — no.

*(Advance to Slide 2)*

**Transformative development** is development that changes systems and institutions — not just statistics on a government dashboard.

It rests on five key pillars:
- **Inclusive Growth** — prosperity that reaches every citizen
- **Environmental Sustainability** — growth that doesn't cost the planet
- **Human Development** — education, health, and dignity
- **Structural Change** — shifting how economies and societies are organised
- **And Institutional Change** — reforming the bodies that govern our lives

Today, we'll look at how India is pursuing these five pillars through the lens of the **UN Sustainable Development Goals**, and the distinct roles that both **humanities** and **science & technology** play in that journey.

## 🎤 SPEAKER 2 — Sumit Kumar Manjhi
**Slides: India's SDG Performance**
*⏱ ~1 min 40 sec*

---

Thank you.

So where does India actually stand on the SDGs right now?

*(Advance to Slide 3)*

According to the **4th Edition SDG India Index**, India ranks **99th out of 167 countries**, with an overall SDG score of **71 out of 100**. The global average sits at 67, so India is performing above the global mean — but that doesn't tell the whole story.

Three SDGs remain particularly challenging:

- **SDG 2 — Zero Hunger:** India's Global Hunger Index score is **25.8 in 2025**, classified as a *serious* hunger level. Nearly 1 in 5 children remain undernourished.

- **SDG 5 — Gender Equality:** The Gender Inequality Index stands at **0.437**, reflecting deep gaps in women's political participation, health, and economic opportunity.

- **SDG 10 — Reduced Inequalities:** A Gini Index of **25.5** per World Bank data highlights that economic growth has not been uniform across regions, castes, and communities.

These numbers tell us something important: **progress has been real, but deeply uneven.** Understanding *why* these gaps persist requires more than technology — it requires us to look at the social and cultural fabric of India. That's where the humanities come in.

## 🎤 SPEAKER 3 — Shubham Meena
**Slides: Role of Indian Humanities**
*⏱ ~1 min 40 sec*

---

Thank you.

*(Advance to Slide 4)*

There is a temptation to think that data, apps, and algorithms will solve India's development challenges. But as this slide shows — **while science provides the tools, humanities determine how they are used, and for whom.**

Indian humanities have shaped transformative development in four key ways:

**First, Ethical Governance and Public Policy** — thinkers like Ambedkar, Gandhi, and Amartya Sen gave us the philosophical foundations for inclusive governance. Their ideas are embedded in institutions like NITI Aayog today — directly touching SDGs 16, 10, 5, and 1.

**Second, Understanding Social Inequality** — Sociology and anthropology revealed the structural causes of poverty. These disciplines directly informed landmark schemes like **MGNREGA**, reservation policies, and tribal rights legislation.

**Third, Gender Equality and Social Justice** — Gender studies exposed patriarchal norms and gave policymakers the language and evidence to design schemes like **Beti Bachao Beti Padhao**, Sukanya Samriddhi Yojana, and PM Ujjwala Yojana.

**And fourth, Cultural Heritage** — history, archaeology, and cultural studies support sustainable tourism and creative industries, preserving community livelihoods through bodies like the **Archaeological Survey of India** and IGNCA.

In short — the humanities are not a soft addition to development. They are foundational to it.

## 🎤 SPEAKER 4 — Parikshit Sharma
**Slides: Impact of Science & Technology on SDGs**
*⏱ ~1 min 40 sec*

---

Thank you.

*(Advance to Slide 5)*

Now let's turn to how Science and Technology are directly accelerating India's SDG performance.

Across five domains, India has deployed technology at scale:

**Education — SDG 4:** Digital platforms and online databases have extended quality education to remote regions, bridging the urban-rural divide in learning access.

**Healthcare — SDG 3:** **Ayushman Bharat**, digital health records, and the National Health Mission have expanded affordable care to hundreds of millions of previously underserved citizens.

**Agriculture — SDG 2:** Schemes like **PM-KISAN**, climate-resilient crop varieties, and soil health technology have improved farmer resilience and productivity, directly addressing food security.

**Clean Energy — SDGs 7 and 13:** India's **Green Hydrogen Mission** alongside solar and wind expansion are driving the fossil fuel transition and reducing emissions at national scale.

**And Digital Governance — SDGs 9 and 16:** Perhaps most transformatively, **Aadhaar, UPI, and India's Digital Public Infrastructure** have expanded financial inclusion and brought transparent, direct-benefit governance to the last mile.

These are not incremental improvements. These are systemic shifts. But technology, as we've argued, needs humanistic direction to be truly transformative. Our next speaker will show how.

## 🎤 SPEAKER 5 — Ayush Kumar
**Slides: Action Plan**
*⏱ ~1 min 45 sec*

---

Thank you.

*(Advance to Slide 6)*

So the question is: how do we bring science, technology, and humanities together into a coherent strategy? Our action plan proposes five convergence points.

**One — SDG Localisation through Data and Community Knowledge:** Pair SDG Index analytics with ethnographic research to capture barriers like caste, language, and historical marginalisation at the district and village level. National data often misses these nuances.

**Two — Ethical Governance of Digital Public Infrastructure:** As Aadhaar, UPI, and AI-assisted welfare systems expand, we must embed **data ethics, digital rights, and privacy law** into their governance frameworks — to prevent algorithmic bias from replicating old inequalities in new digital forms.

**Three — Behavioural Science and Medical Technology:** Combine telemedicine and diagnostic AI with anthropology and psychology to address health-seeking behaviour, vaccination hesitancy, and mental health stigma — challenges that technology alone cannot solve.

**Four — Climate Science and Environmental Humanities:** Alongside the Green Hydrogen Mission and PM-KUSUM, we must integrate **energy justice, land rights, and participatory community dialogue** for those displaced by clean energy transitions.

**And Five — Viksit Bharat 2047:** As India builds toward a 30 trillion dollar economy, we need concurrent investment in historians, philosophers, and social scientists who can interrogate power and articulate rights — so that growth serves all Indians, not just some.

## 🎤 SPEAKER 6 — Kishan Kumar / Siddhant Sinha
**Slides: Conclusion + Thank You**
*⏱ ~1 min 30 sec*

---

Thank you.

*(Advance to Slide 7)*

To conclude — India's journey with the SDGs reflects a story of **uneven but real progress.**

An SDG Index score of 71, a booming digital infrastructure, and transformative programmes like Ayushman Bharat and the Green Hydrogen Mission show genuine institutional will. But persistent hunger, gender inequality, and economic disparity remind us that the work is far from done.

Technology has been an instrument of change — but an instrument is only as good as the values that guide its use.

The SDGs are ultimately a **humanistic commitment**. They ask us not just how fast an economy grows, but *who benefits*, *who is protected*, and *whose dignity is upheld*.

As we noted on this slide — the SDGs are not a technocratic checklist. They are a moral and political commitment to the dignity of every human being.

India's path to Viksit Bharat requires both **technological ambition and humanistic responsibility.** Neither alone is enough.
